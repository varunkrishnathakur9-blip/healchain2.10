ABSTRACT
Federated learning (FL) is a paradigm that allows multiple entities, particularly in sen-
sitive domains like smart healthcare, to collaboratively train a global machine learning
model without sharing their raw private data. Although FL addresses initial privacy con-
cerns, it raises new risks, such as the potential for reconstructing sensitive information
from shared model updates (gradients). Furthermore, most existing privacy-preserving
FL (PPFL) solutions, which rely on complex cryptographic methods, face issues like
computational overhead and a critical lack of robust, verifiable fairness mechanisms to
incentivize high-quality participation and prevent ”free-riding”. To address these multi-
faceted concerns, we propose HealChain, an enhanced framework built upon the Efficient
and Secure Blockchain-based Federated Learning (ESB-FL) architecture. HealChain re-
tains the efficiency benefits of Non-Interactive Designated Decryptor Functional Encryp-
tion (NDD-FE) and Decentralized Gradient Compression (DGC), while introducing three
core innovations: (1) an Escrow-based Smart Contract Mechanism that locks task rewards
on-chain to guarantee payment upon successful task completion; (2) a Commit-Reveal
Verification Protocol that ensures the Task Publisher’s model accuracy requirement re-
mains immutable and verifiable; and (3) a Gradient-Norm Based Contribution Scoring
function (∥∆′
i∥2) to accurately measure the quality of miner updates for fair, propor-
tional reward distribution. This architecturally modular system comprises seven distinct
phases from task publishing to reward distribution. We conduct a comprehensive security
analysis and outline planned experimental work to demonstrate that our new framework
provides enhanced security, verifiable fairness, and high communication efficiency.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Federated Learning (FL) is an emerging technology that allows users to train a centralized
model on separate datasets stored by different entities [1]. FL is a distributed machine
learning paradigm where data remains decentralized across clients, such as hospitals,
financial institutions, or IoT devices. Instead of sharing raw data, only model updates
(gradients or weights) are exchanged, making this approach increasingly prevalent in
business and society. Its applications in sensitive fields like healthcare drive reforms in
areas such as medical diagnosis and treatment and disease risk factor prediction.
The rapid growth of FL benefits from two main drivers: (1) applying machine learning
technologies to distributed data scenarios, and (2) providing data privacy protection
during the data application process [2]. This decentralized nature preserves data privacy,
enables cross-organization collaboration, and helps comply with privacy regulations (e.g.,
GDPR, HIPAA). The objective is to provide a secure, efficient, and fair platform for
sensitive applications like health care centers.
1.1
Problem Motivation and Background
Although FL brings great benefits, it also raises new privacy concerns in practical appli-
cations [3]. Recent research has shown that FL may not always offer sufficient privacy
protection for local training datasets. This is mainly because some malicious adversaries
may extract sensitive information about the training datasets (e.g., private medical data)
from the model parameters, such as weights or gradients. For instance, the member-
ship inference attack could construct a series of shadow models through local gradi-
ents to reconstruct original data samples. Furthermore, FL systems are vulnerable to
malicious participants through data poisoning (corrupting local data) and model poi-
soning (manipulating local updates) attacks. Such potential privacy leakage risks in FL
may become a serious obstacle for practical and privacy-sensitive scenarios, particularly
healthcare.

1.2
Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (PPFL)
and Techniques
To address these privacy concerns, Privacy-Preserving FL (PPFL) is introduced by
applying specific privacy-enhancing techniques (PETs) before model updates are shared
[4].
The PPFL schemes can be categorized into two types according to the privacy-
preserving methods:
1.2.1
Non-Crypto-Based Methods
These methods, primarily Differential Privacy (DP), rely on modifying, restricting,
or managing data rather than using encryption [5].
DP provides privacy guarantees
by adding controlled noise to data or model parameters.
While offering more effi-
cient performance, these methods often introduce a trade-off: it is hard to achieve high
model accuracy and strong privacy simultaneously, as the effect of adding noise may
be unknown. Other non-crypto methods include Anonymization (stripping PII) and
Pseudonymization (replacing identifiers with consistent tokens).
1.2.2
Crypto-Based Methods
These techniques, such as Homomorphic Encryption (HE) or Secure Multi-Party
Computation (MPC), use cryptographic algorithms to secure data during storage,
transmission, or computation [6]. These methods can provide stronger privacy protec-
tion while retaining the original accuracy, enabling computation on encrypted data. How-
ever, despite providing strong guarantees, these solutions face significant computational
and communication costs, which may hinder the wide application of crypto-based
PPFL schemes.
For example, schemes adopting fully homomorphic encryption tech-
nology may incur vast computational and communication overhead. Zero-Knowledge
Proofs (ZKP) are also used to prove knowledge of a fact without revealing the actual
data.
1.3
The Fair Payment Challenge and Project Moti-
vation
In addition to efficiency, achieving a fair exchange between task publishers and task
participants (miners) in the FL framework is an extremely meaningful challenge [1]. A
financial incentive mechanism is typically needed to motivate task participants to actively
train models. However, how to ensure fairness of both parties in the trading process is

3
still an open problem. Participants may be motivated to submit models lazily or with
low accuracy if paid in advance, or the publisher may refuse payment if they receive the
model first. An effective FL framework must ensure that the FL task can be completed
correctly and each participant who participates can obtain the task rewards timely.
A native solution is a trusted arbiter, but this largely eliminates the benefits of this
distributed framework. Blockchain technology, proven effective at financial services,
offers a decentralized alternative to build a decentralized, healthy FL system with incen-
tive mechanisms. However, existing blockchain-based schemes often fail to balance data
privacy protection with the fair payment of rewards.
1.4
Project Contributions: The HealChain Frame-
work
To comprehensively address the issues of efficiency, data privacy, and verifiable
fair payment, we propose the HealChain framework, an enhanced system built upon
the Efficient and Secure Blockchain-based Federated Learning (ESB-FL) architecture.
HealChain is designed to overcome limitations in existing crypto-based PPFL, specifically
computational overhead and the lack of fair incentives.
Our primary contributions are the integration of three novel, robust mechanisms into
the ESB-FL architecture:
1. Escrow-based Smart Contract Mechanism: Ensures the Task Publisher (TP)
deposits the reward (R) into an on-chain escrow upon task initialization, securing
funds and preventing payment disputes.
2. Commit-Reveal Verification Protocol: Requires the TP to commit to the re-
quired model accuracy (accreq) using a hash commitment (commitHash = H(accreq∥nonceTP)).
This ensures immutable task requirements verified by the smart contract upon re-
veal.
3. Gradient-Norm Based Contribution Scoring: Assesses miners’ local contri-
butions by computing the L2 norm (∥∆′
i∥2) of their compressed gradient updates.
This verifiable metric proxies contribution quality, enabling proportional reward
distribution and actively discouraging free-riding.
In summary, HealChain ensures that a highly accurate model can be trained while pro-
tecting the privacy of local training data, all while supporting verifiable, fair payment
between the task publisher and all participants.

Chapter 2
Literature Review and Foundational
Framework
This chapter reviews relevant academic literature concerning Privacy-Preserving Fed-
erated Learning (PPFL) and blockchain integration, identifying the research gaps
addressed by our proposed system. It concludes by detailing the architecture of the base
Efficient and Secure Blockchain-Based Federated Learning (ESB-FL) frame-
work, upon which HealChain is built.
2.1
Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (PPFL)
PPFL aims to collaboratively train a global model while strongly preserving data privacy
[1].
The current landscape of PPFL solutions can be broadly divided based on the
underlying privacy-preserving mechanisms.
2.1.1
Non-Cryptographic Privacy Mechanisms
Non-cryptographic technologies, primarily reliant on Differential Privacy (DP), pro-
vide privacy guarantees by adding controlled noise to local data or model parameters
[2]. While this offers competitive efficiency, applying DP results in an inherent trade-off:
achieving a high level of privacy often requires significant noise injection, which inversely
affects model accuracy and utility [3, 4].
Consequently, non-crypto methods struggle
to satisfy applications that demand both rigorous privacy guarantees and high model
accuracy.
2.1.2
Cryptographic Privacy Mechanisms
In contrast, cryptographic technologies, including Homomorphic Encryption (HE)
and Secure Multi-Party Computation (MPC), provide stronger privacy guarantees
while retaining the original model accuracy [5]. HE schemes allow computation directly
4


--- PAGE 14 ---
5
on encrypted data, preventing information leakage throughout the aggregation process
[6]. MPC protocols enable distributed participants to securely compute a joint objective
function without revealing their individual inputs [6].
However, these solutions face significant practical hurdles: HE schemes often incur
vast computational overhead [2], and MPC protocols frequently demand substantial
communication costs [6] due to complex cryptographic mechanisms or intricate multi-
round interactions.
This low efficiency hinders the wide deployment of crypto-based
PPFL schemes.
2.2
Blockchain Integration and Fair Incentive Mech-
anisms
Blockchain technology is increasingly adopted in FL frameworks due to its decentral-
ization, verifiability, and immutability, often replacing the need for a single trusted
central server [5].
2.2.1
Incentive Mechanisms and Fair Payment Gaps
While blockchain acts as an immutable distributed ledger, integrating an effective incen-
tive mechanism that ensures fair exchange remains challenging [1]. Many existing works
focus on achieving fair assessment of a participant’s technical contribution, but they often
neglect the fairness of the payment procedure itself.
For example, if rewards are paid upfront, participants may submit lazily trained,
low-accuracy models (free-riding). Conversely, if the Task Publisher (TP) receives the
model first, they may refuse payment. An ideal framework must ensure that the task
can be completed correctly and that each participant obtains the task rewards timely.
Traditional blockchain-based schemes often fail to balance data privacy protection with
fair payment of rewards.
2.2.2
Survey of Related Blockchain-FL Frameworks
Several blockchain-based FL frameworks have attempted to tackle these issues, but they
each present distinct trade-offs:
• TAPFed [2]: Employs Threshold Functional Encryption (TFE) for secure aggre-
gation. Merit: Superior security. Demerit: Lacks comprehensive, explicit incentive
and payment logic.
• Federify [5]: Utilizes zkSNARKs and smart contracts for model verifiability. Merit:
Transparency, verifiability. Demerit: Heavy proof generation and high gas cost.

6
• FLoBC [4]: Features a decentralized consensus mechanism with validator/trainer
ratios. Merit: Byzantine tolerance. Demerit: Adds extra validation overhead and
provides less formal privacy guarantees.
• PEFL [3]: Uses local Differential Privacy and anomaly detection. Merit: Balanced
privacy and efficient defense. Demerit: Core reliance on DP results in model accu-
racy loss and lacks a robust, cryptographically verifiable fair payment mechanism.
2.3
The Base Framework: ESB-FL
Our HealChain project is anchored in the principles established by the Efficient and
Secure Blockchain-Based Federated Learning (ESB-FL) framework [1]. ESB-FL
was specifically designed to balance security, efficiency, and initial payment feasibility.
2.3.1
Non-Interactive Designated Decryptor Functional Encryp-
tion (NDD-FE)
ESB-FL introduced NDD-FE to protect the privacy of training data while maintaining
high communication performance [1]. This scheme is a lightweight cryptographic tool [1]
supporting inner-product computation [6] and ensures:
1. Non-Interactive Key Derivation: Eliminates the need for repeated multi-round
communication between the key generator and the data encryptor [1], significantly
lowering communication costs.
2. Designated Decryptor: Only the specified Aggregation Node (A) holds the de-
cryption function key (skFE) [1], preventing all other parties from accessing indi-
vidual or aggregated updates.
The aggregated result is recovered using the Baby-Step Giant-Step (BSGS) al-
gorithm [1], with security resting on the Computational Diffie-Hellman (CDH) as-
sumption [1].
2.3.2
Decentralized Gradient Compression (DGC) and Blockchain
Structure
Decentralized Gradient Compression (DGC): The DGC algorithm [1] is imple-
mented at the local training stage. It minimizes communication costs [1] by compressing
gradient updates (∆i) into a sparse, quantized update (∆′
i) prior to NDD-FE encryption,
substantially mitigating the communication bottleneck inherent in FL systems [1].

Designed Blockchain Structure for Payment: ESB-FL modifies the traditional
block header to bind task performance and participant information to the immutable
ledger. The header is enhanced with digital signatures for the task information (σ1) and

are released only if the trained model meets the Task Publisher’s requirements [1], laying

In conclusion, HealChain builds upon these efficiency and foundational payment
structures by enhancing verifiability and contribution fairness mechanisms, ensuring privacy-
preserving and incentivized federated learning.

This chapter provides an expanded introduction to the foundational technologies and

3.1
Blockchain
Blockchain can be regarded as a decentralized, distributed database composed of a
chronological collection of blocks linked by cryptographic hash pointers.
This chain-
like structure guarantees immutability, ensuring that once a transaction or block is
recorded, it cannot be tampered with retrospectively without invalidating all subsequent
blocks.
Consensus mechanisms, such as Proof-of-Stake (PoS) utilized in the participant
selection phase, ensure that all network nodes agree on the validity of new blocks before
they are added.
In the context of HealChain, the blockchain serves several critical functions:
• Maintaining the task registry.
• Securely storing immutable commitments (commitHash) to required accuracy.
• Verifiably logging contribution scores.
• Executing the pre-defined logic of the Escrow Smart Contract for fair, condi-
tional reward distribution.
This decentralized design eliminates the dependency on a central trusted arbiter while
ensuring transparency and fairness in collaborative learning.

3.2
Hard Problem Assumption
The security and privacy guarantees of the Functional Encryption (FE) scheme are rooted
in the computational intractability of specific mathematical problems. For the NDD-FE
scheme utilized in HealChain, the confidentiality of local training gradients and the
security of the inner-product calculation are directly dependent on the difficulty of solving
the Computational Diffie-Hellman (CDH) assumption.
3.2.1
Definition: Computational Diffie-Hellman (CDH) Assump-
tion
Let G be a cyclic group of prime order p, generated by an element g, typically derived
from an efficient algorithm GGen(1λ), where λ is the security parameter. Given the group
parameters (G, p, g) and two derived group elements (ga, gb) ∈G, where the exponents a
and b are randomly chosen in Z∗
p, the CDH problem is to compute the element gab.
We assume that the advantage of solving the CDH problem is negligible, confirming
its suitability as a secure foundation.
3.2.2
Alignment with NDD-FE Privacy
The CDH assumption is critical because it ensures that unauthorized parties cannot
deduce the random mask applied to the encrypted gradients. The random component
used in the encryption,
ri = H1(pkski
TP, ctr, T),
relies on computing a secure hash of an element where the exponent is derived from the
product of the Task Publisher’s public key exponent (sTP) and the Miner’s secret key
exponent (si).
Since
pkski
TP = (gsTP)si = gsTPsi,
recovering the randomized component ri without knowing both sTP and si requires solving
a CDH instance. This guarantees that the encrypted gradient Ui remains computationally
indistinguishable from a random element for any unauthorized party.
Furthermore, while the aggregated result is recovered using the Baby-Step Giant-
Step (BSGS) algorithm, this is only feasible because the numeric aggregated value
⟨∆′, y⟩is a relatively small integer recovered from the exponent of g, distinguishing it
from the large, random, CDH-hard exponents used for masking. This achieves a balance
between efficiency and security.

3.3
Non-Interactive Designated Decryptor Functional
Encryption (NDD-FE)
Functional Encryption (FE) allows a party to derive a function key (sky) that permits
a decryptor to compute a specific function ⟨x, y⟩on the encrypted input x, without
revealing x.
3.3.1
NDD-FE: Non-Interactive and Designated Security
HealChain adopts NDD-FE, an enhancement designed to overcome the communication
overhead of traditional FE (by achieving non-interactive key generation) and ensuring
security by designating only the Aggregation Node (A) as the sole decryptor.
The system uses a public key pk1 and secret key sk1 for the Task Publisher (TP),
and key pairs (pki, ski) for miners and (pkA, skA) for the Aggregator. The algorithms are
instantiated as follows:

3.4
Federated Learning (FL) Framework
A classic Federated Learning (FL) cycle involves collaborative training across multiple
miners (Mi) holding diverse local datasets (Di), coordinated by an Aggregation Node (A).
The core goal is to generate a robust global model W without centralizing proprietary
data.
The iterative process relies on three primary phases:
1. Initialization: The cycle begins when the Task Publisher (TP) broadcasts the
task parameters and the initial global model W0.
2. Local Training: Each miner i ∈{1, . . . , h} downloads Wt, trains it using their
local data Di, and computes their raw local gradient update ∆i. In HealChain,
this phase is augmented by Decentralized Gradient Compression (DGC) to
compute the sparse update ∆′
i, followed by the NDD-FE Encryption (as detailed

and protection of the privacy-sensitive local update ∆i.
3. Secure Aggregation and Update: The Aggregation Node A collects the en-
crypted updates {Ui}. Leveraging the NDD-FE scheme, A securely computes the
aggregated, decentralized update ⟨∆′, y⟩. This aggregated gradient is then decom-
pressed and applied to the existing global model Wt, resulting in the updated model
Wt+1. This process continues until W converges or meets the committed accuracy
requirement (accreq).

Overview of the ESB-FL Framework
The Efficient and Secure Blockchain-Based Federated Learning (ESB-FL) framework was
proposed to address the fundamental challenges of privacy, efficiency, and fair payment
in Federated Learning (FL) systems. It functions by combining a novel Non-Interactive
Designated Decryptor Functional Encryption (NDD-FE) scheme with the Decentralized
Gradient Compression (DGC) algorithm, all orchestrated through an elaborately designed
blockchain. This architecture ensures strong data privacy for local training gradients
while minimizing communication overhead [1].
Crucially, ESB-FL modifies the traditional blockchain structure to bind task informa-
tion and model performance to the ledger using digital signatures, thereby establishing a
basic framework for conditional reward payment where participants are guaranteed com-
pensation if, and only if, the trained model satisfies the Task Publisher’s requirements.
This foundation of efficient, cryptographically secured aggregation and immutable, condi-
tional recording serves as the launchpad for the enhanced fairness mechanisms introduced
in HealChain.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Federated Learning (FL) offers a powerful paradigm for collaboratively training high-
precision models, which is particularly relevant in highly sensitive sectors like smart
healthcare where data privacy is paramount. However, the practical viability of existing
Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (PPFL) solutions is severely constrained by per-
sistent challenges, namely high computational and communication overhead stemming
from complex cryptography, and a fundamental lack of verifiable, proportional fairness
mechanisms to incentivize honest participation.
This report introduced HealChain, an enhanced framework built upon the Efficient
and Secure Blockchain-Based Federated Learning (ESB-FL) architecture. HealChain was
explicitly designed to create a fully decentralized, trustless, and highly efficient system
that overcomes the shortcomings in fairness and payment integrity found in existing
solutions. By retaining the established security of the NDD-FE scheme and integrating
three core enhancements, HealChain provides robust guarantees across security, efficiency,
and financial equity.
Summary of Contributions and Achievements
The success of HealChain rests on its novel integration of cryptographic primitives and
specialized smart contract logic across the entire workflow (M1–M7).
First, the implementation of an Escrow-based Smart Contract Mechanism (M1)
is the core financial safeguard. By requiring the Task Publisher (TP) to deposit the full
reward (R) into a secure, on-chain escrow upon task initialization, we effectively eliminate
the risk of payment default and secure the miners’ incentives immediately.
Second, to prevent the TP from arbitrarily shifting goalposts, we introduced the
Commit–Reveal Verification Protocol. The TP commits a hash (commitHash) of
the required accuracy (accreq) before training starts (M1), and the smart contract rigor-
ously validates this commitment upon finalization (M7), ensuring payment is conditional
only on the original, agreed-upon quality standard.

The most critical enhancement for sustainability is the Gradient-Norm Based
Contribution Scoring (M3/M7). This method calculates a miner’s contribution based
on the L2 norm (∥∆′
i∥2) of their cryptographically compressed gradient update. This
objective metric is committed to the blockchain, verified upon reward distribution, and
used to calculate payment that is strictly proportional to the quality of work submitted.
This measure actively discourages “free-riding,” guaranteeing that computational effort
is correctly compensated.
Beyond fairness, HealChain maintains superior efficiency.
The integration of De-
centralized Gradient Compression (DGC) significantly reduces the data volume
transmitted, while the NDD-FE scheme leverages the CDH Assumption to ensure non-
interactive, lightweight secure aggregation. This design avoids the massive computational
latency typical of Homomorphic Encryption and the communication complexity of Multi-
Party Computation, confirming HealChain’s scalability for practical application.
Finally, the Miner Verification Consensus (M5/M6) provides a decentralized
check on the Aggregator’s final block submission, ensuring the integrity of the published
model and preventing single-point failure attacks before the final payment is triggered.
Future Research Directions
While HealChain provides a complete, robust, and verifiable framework, its foundation
allows for exciting avenues of future research. We intend to expand the capability of
our system by developing a formal on-chain governance mechanism capable of resolving
complex disputes and collusion scenarios that go beyond the current cryptographic checks.
Furthermore, exploring alternative, potentially more nuanced contribution metrics
compatible with NDD-FE will be crucial for capturing the true value of each participant’s
update. Finally, extending the Functional Encryption scheme to support a broader range
of non-linear functions would enable HealChain to secure a wider variety of modern deep
learning models, solidifying its position as a truly general-purpose and efficient PPFL
solution.
